17. Glossary (Search-Friendly Definitions)

Basal metabolic rate: The energy used at rest to maintain essential functions such as breathing, circulation, and temperature regulation.
Calorie: A unit of energy. In nutrition, calorie typically means kilocalorie, the amount of energy needed to raise one kilogram of water by one degree Celsius.
Glycemic response: The change in blood glucose after eating, influenced by carbohydrate type, fiber, fat, protein, and individual factors.
Inflammation: An immune response that can be protective in the short term but harmful when persistent and excessive.
Resting heart rate: The number of heart beats per minute at rest; it can decrease with improved aerobic fitness.
Blood pressure: The force of blood against artery walls, typically reported as systolic over diastolic.
LDL cholesterol: A cholesterol-carrying particle often associated with atherosclerosis risk when elevated over time.
HDL cholesterol: A cholesterol-carrying particle involved in reverse cholesterol transport; its interpretation depends on context.
Triglycerides: A form of fat in the blood that can rise after eating and with certain dietary patterns and metabolic states.
Insulin sensitivity: How effectively cells respond to insulin; higher sensitivity generally supports better glucose regulation.
Protein synthesis: The process of building proteins from amino acids, important for muscle repair and many bodily structures.
Fiber: A class of carbohydrates not fully digested by humans, supporting gut health and regular bowel movements.
Electrolyte: A mineral that carries electrical charge in fluids, crucial for nerve signaling and muscle contraction.
Circadian rhythm: The body’s approximately 24-hour timing system that influences sleep, hormones, and metabolism.
Sleep efficiency: The proportion of time in bed actually spent asleep; low efficiency can indicate fragmented sleep.
VO2 max: A measure of maximal oxygen uptake, often used as an index of cardiorespiratory fitness.
Progressive overload: Gradually increasing training demands to drive adaptation in strength, endurance, or skill.
RPE: Rating of perceived exertion, a subjective scale used to estimate intensity of effort during exercise.
Micronutrient: A vitamin or mineral required in small amounts for health and normal physiology.
Nutrient density: How many beneficial nutrients a food provides relative to its energy content.
Satiety: The feeling of fullness and satisfaction after eating that influences how soon one eats again.


18. Scenario Notes (Generic)

Scenario 1: A person reports afternoon sleepiness most days. Common contributors include insufficient sleep duration, inconsistent bed and wake times, heavy midday meals, dehydration, and high caffeine intake that disrupts nighttime sleep. A typical first step is to review sleep schedule consistency, morning light exposure, and caffeine timing. If snoring, witnessed pauses in breathing, or persistent daytime sleepiness occurs, screening for sleep apnea is often appropriate.

Scenario 2: A person begins resistance training after a long break. Early soreness is common, especially after high volume or unfamiliar exercises. Gradual progression, adequate protein intake, and sufficient sleep can support adaptation. Pain that is sharp, localized, or associated with swelling or loss of function warrants evaluation.

Scenario 3: A person increases dietary fiber rapidly and experiences bloating. A common approach is to raise fiber gradually, distribute it across meals, and increase water intake. Some individuals are sensitive to fermentable fibers, and they may tolerate certain sources better than others.

Scenario 4: A person wants to reduce cardiovascular risk. Common evidence-aligned levers include more frequent aerobic activity, modest weight loss when appropriate, more plant-forward meals with unsaturated fats, reduced intake of trans fats, adequate sleep, and avoidance of smoking.

These scenarios are intentionally generic and are included to improve retrieval for question-style queries.
